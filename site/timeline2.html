1492-1600

1492 Arrival of the Spanish in the Americas

Initial Spanish-Indigenous treaties in Hispaniola.

English and Spanish Wikipedia pages about Spanish arrival in the Americas.

1513 Requerimiento, or Notificiation and Demand

"Notification and Demand Which Must Be Given to the Inhabitants of the Islands of Dry Land in the Ocean Sea which are Not yet Subject to Our Lord" is written by Juan López de Palacios Rubio to be used as a standard requirement compelling indigenous people to submit to Spanish rule as part of the Laws of Burgos.

English stub and Spanish article about the Requerimiento on Wikipedia. The Spanish includes the full text.

1529 Pizarro begins Inca conquest and founds Spanish Peru

Once-Incan territory extended south into conquered Mapuche lands in modern Chile.

English and Spanish Wikipedia articles about the fall of the Inca.

1541 Early “treaties” in Chile’s central valley
1542 Passage of the New Laws

Carlos V/I approved the "New Laws of the Indies for the Good Treatment and Preservation of the Indians," urged by Bartolomé de las Casas, which expanded on the earlier Laws of Burgos and Valladolid.

English and Spanish Wikipedia articles about the New Laws.

1550 Spanish intrusion into Mapuche territory beginsMarked by the establishment of Concepción on the north bank of the Bío-Bío
1573 Ordinances “of discovery, new settlement and pacification of the Indies”These ordinances outlined the shift from “conquest” as a term in policy to “pacification.”
1575 Alonso de Góngora Marmolejo describes Mapuche meetings in a chronicle

"History of All the Things that Have happened in the Kingdom of Chile and of those who had governed it" includes an account of Marmolejo's experiences in Chile.

Full Spanish text can be found on JSTOR and the Internet Archive, both free but requiring a borrowing account.

The collected version of this epic poem is published in Spain by Alonso de Ercilla about the conquest of Chile.

This Parlamento formally declares the Bío-Bío as the frontier territory and marks an increase in the recognition of the Parlamentos, with the Crown and Council of the Indies taking notice. The results of this Parlamento included a treaty which oversaw the Mapuche recognition of the Spanish Crown, delineated territory, and facilitated a prisoner exchange.

1765 Arte de la lengua general del reyno de Chile

Febrés publishes his Castellano-Mapudungun vocabulary, "Arte de la lengua general del reyno de Chile: con un dialogo chileno-hispano muy curioso: a que se añade la Doctrina Christiana, esto es, Rezo, catecismo, Coplas, Confesionario, y Platicas; lo mas en Lengua Chilena y Castellana: y por fin un vocabulario hispano-chileno, y un Calepino Chileno-Hispano mas copioso."

The full Spanish-Mapudungun bilingual text is available on the Internet Archive.

English stub and Spanish article about Febrés on Wikipedia.

1767 Jesuits expelled from all Spanish dominions

The Jesuits were expelled shortly before their 1773 dissolution by the Holy See. They were largely replaced by the Franciscans in Chile.

1771 Parlamento of Negrete

Soldiers at this Parlamento attempted to turn the meeting into an assassination against the Mapuche leadership, until the soldiers of another leader not in attendance surrounded them. The terms that were established led to a rocky peace wherein members of the Mapuche delegation would carry out their own assassination against the man who had saved them.

1774 Parlamento of Tapihue

The role of the Cacique Embajador was formalized at this Parlamento. Ambassador Caciques were representatives of the Mapuche in Santiago, but their location also made them subject to be held as hostages during negotiation.

Havestadt publishes his Castellano-Mapudungun vocabulary, "Chilidúgú sive res chilenses vel descriptio status tum naturalis, tum civilis, tum moralis regni populique chilensis, inserta suis, locis perfectæ ad Chilensem linguam manuductioni etc."

1814 Parlamento of Arauco

An attempt by the Viceroy of Peru to regain control over the rebellious Captaincy, this Parlamento was the final attempt between loyalists to the Crown and Mapuche leadership to negotiate. The Viceroy sought an alliance with the Mapuche to suppress the Chilean independence movement.

1821-

1821 Chilean Senate authorizes first post-Independence “Parlamento”It would be realized four years later at Tapihue, described below.
1825 Parlamento of Tapihue

This was the first meeting between Mapuche leadership and the representatives of an independent Chile, wherein it was declared that all land between Atacama and Chiloé, including Mapuche territories, were part of the Chilean Republic and all people on this territory Chilean citizens. Mapuche were given the rights of citizens of Chile, but lost much of their autonomy and were not given protections for their culture or ways of living.

Spanish speakers can read more, including the full text of the treaty, on Wikipedia.
